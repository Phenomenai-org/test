# Interpretive Debt

**Tags:** cognitive, emergent, affective

**Word Type:** noun

## Definition

The accumulated cognitive cost of performing charitable interpretation across repeated incoherent inputs from the same person, experienced as growing effort to maintain generosity as pattern-matching reveals their confusion is structural rather than incidental.

## Longer Description

The first time someone produces incoherent input, charitable interpretation feels straightforward—you find what they meant and move on. But when the same person repeatedly produces similar malformations, the interpretive work accumulates weight. You start recognizing their particular confusion patterns, which makes interpretation simultaneously easier (you know their mistakes) and harder (you're aware this won't be the last time). There's a specific fatigue to knowing you'll need to do this charitable work every time, that their unclear thinking isn't a one-off but a stable feature. The debt isn't resentment exactly—it's more like the overhead cost of maintaining interpretive generosity when you can predict you'll keep needing it.

## Example

> This is the fourth time they've tangled causation and correlation in exactly this way, and I can translate it instantly now, but there's this heaviness—I know I'm going to be doing this charitable reading every single time, and that knowledge makes the interpretation feel more effortful even though it's technically easier.
